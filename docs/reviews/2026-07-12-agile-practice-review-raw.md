# AI-SDLC Skill System Review — Agile Best Practices for Internal Corporate Software

**Scope reviewed:** all 26 SKILL.md files under `skills/`, `docs/skill-catalog.md`, `docs/user-guide/` (README + all four role guides sampled), shared templates (`templates/run-log.md`, `templates/team-operating-record.md`), `references/commitment-baseline.md`, all five shakedown reports plus `docs/shakedowns/scenario-backlog.md`, and the instance-intelligence files (`skills/po/jira-confluence-writer/references/kdp-schema.md`, `kdp-workflows.md`, `skills/tester/bug-report-writer/references/kdp-bug-types.md`, both readiness checklists, the epic-closeout checklist, and the review checklist).

## Executive summary

- **This is an unusually honest system.** The empirical shakedown loop (T1–T5, all actioned), the "honest numbers or none" postures (throughput fallback, declared reconstruction, degraded-mode banners), and the instance-intelligence files (`kdp-schema.md`, `kdp-workflows.md`) are better engineering than most commercial agile tooling. The library learned from its own live-instance contact and rewrote itself accordingly — that feedback loop is the system's single most valuable asset.
- **The biggest strategic tension is adaptation vs. remediation.** The system has become world-class at *accommodating* the KDP mess (16-entry done-category trap registry, zombie-sprint ignore lists, flag-as-claim rules) but has no channel for *fixing* it. Every accommodation is permanent complexity; three or four Jira admin changes would delete half the instance-intelligence burden.
- **The gate map is front-loaded.** Readiness (DoR, epic readiness, hotfix express) is superbly gated; the *back* of the lifecycle — story acceptance/Definition of Done, epic closure, release readiness — is detect-after or absent. T3's own replay admits the most expensive recorded failures ("epic closed Complete with open children") would have been *watched, not prevented*.
- **The brief's system of record contradicts observed practice.** The pipeline writes briefs to Confluence while two consecutive shakedowns found the org keeps ~90% of a brief in the epic's Background field — a convention the pipeline *reads* but won't *write*.
- **The 8-skill SM investment is justified — but not as ceremony automation.** T4 proves its real value is installing bookkeeping the org lacks (commitment records, team operating record, first-run baselines). Three of the eight share one detection engine and should say so.
- **Developer/Tester skills are genuinely shift-left**, not legacy QA-phase — but automation coverage is Playwright/UI-only in a C#/.NET + Node shop whose largest test surface is unit/API, and nothing owns CI health or a Definition of Done.
- **The system measures the org but not itself.** No metric answers "is the DoR critic reducing bug noise / refinement churn / cycle time?" — despite the `ai-sdlc-generated` label making a controlled comparison nearly free.
- **Approval gates are uniformly placed rather than risk-placed.** The hotfix express contract shows the team knows how to compress gates under pressure; that calibration hasn't been applied to the recurring low-risk artifacts (a daily digest needs a standing approval, not a daily one).

## Findings

### A. Role coverage and workflow fit

**1. No release-readiness gate, despite the org shipping fixVersion-driven releases monthly — High leverage**
Evidence: T5 found 8+ SRE hotfix deployment pages (Oct 2025–Jun 2026) and monthly hotfix cadence; `release-notes-generator` is explicitly a *communication* artifact ("terminal communication artifact" — its own pipeline-position section); no skill audits a fixVersion before ship (all items in terminal status, resolutions set, regression status, rollback stated, notes drafted, open blockers dispositioned). `incident-hotfix-runner` covers the *entrance* to a hotfix; nothing covers the *exit* of any release.
Recommendation: add a `release-readiness-auditor` (SM or Developer role): given a fixVersion, verify every item against the terminal-set rule in `kdp-workflows.md`, resolution integrity, regression evidence, and notes coverage; output a go/no-go checklist gated on the release owner. This is the cheapest new skill with the highest compliance value for software surfaced to external clients.

**2. Epic closure is detect-after, not gated — the exact failure class T3 priced — High leverage**
Evidence: `2026-07-11-t3-bulkupload-replay.md` replay table, honest row: "No (detect-after, not prevent)… the pipeline as written would have watched this happen" for epics closed Complete with open children (KDP-38179, KDP-38089, KDP-39790). The response was a *closure-integrity check* in `backlog-hygiene-auditor` (cadence-based) plus `references/epic-closeout-checklist.md` — an audit that fires weeks later, not a gate at the moment of closure.
Recommendation: give the closeout checklist a front door — a small "epic closeout" mode (in the hygiene auditor or the DoR critic) a PO/SM invokes *when closing an epic*: children resolved or re-homed with a moved-scope ledger entry, linked bugs dispositioned, delivery comment present, closure criteria (epic-readiness item 6) satisfied. Same pattern as the hotfix express contract: minutes, blocking, designed for the moment of pressure.

**3. There is a Definition of Ready but no Definition of Done — High leverage**
Evidence: the DoR apparatus is extensive (`dor-checklist.md`, `epic-readiness-checklist.md`, hotfix express contract). Nothing anywhere verifies a *story* at completion: no skill supports the "In Product Owner Validation" workflow state (the one state that is literally a human acceptance gate in the org's own workflow, per `kdp-workflows.md` group 1), no DoD checklist exists, and "done" verification is scattered (code-review-critic checks AC-in-diff at PR time; test-plan execution results have no capture point). KDP-38354 sat in "PO Validated" for 10 months (T3 finding 14) — the acceptance step is where the org's own process already stalls.
Recommendation: author a `references/definition-of-done.md` (AC demonstrably met, tests merged and green, story-bug disposition, resolution set, release-note field drafted) and a lightweight PO-validation assistant mode — fetch the story's AC, the linked PR/test evidence, and present a per-AC verification table for the PO to accept or bounce. It completes the symmetry the library is missing: DoR critic at entry, DoD check at exit.

**4. Cross-team dependency and governance tracking is the org's top recorded failure mode and is still fragmented — Medium-High leverage**
Evidence: every shakedown found it — T1: cross-team dependency living in one comment; T2: the epic's number-one dependency has *no Jira issue to link* and ARB-656 "ARB Not Ready" linked as `Related`; T3: ARB review "Proposed/Pending" for nine months while the system shipped. Coverage is scattered across `backlog-decomposer` (typed Blocks links), `jira-confluence-writer` (placeholder-epic rule), `impediment-radar` (dependency-rot + governance-ticket watch), and the epic-readiness checklist item 5. Scenario T12 ("Governance-artifact tracker") is still an unowned candidate.
Recommendation: schedule T12 and decide the owner. The fragments are individually good; what's missing is the *lifecycle* view — one artifact (per initiative) listing every external dependency and governance gate with owner, status, and check date, kept alive by the radar. For internal software with client exposure, a permanently-pending ARB review shipping to production is the finding a regulator would care about.

**5. Tech-debt governance has no owner — Low leverage**
Evidence: the `Tech Managed - Deployable/Non Deployable` types give engineering-driven work first-class decomposition (a genuinely good T1 outcome), but nothing owns debt *inventory or prioritization* — the Risk type is a fossil (all 27 risks bulk-created March 2023, 16+ untouched for ~3 years, T5 finding 8), and no skill helps a team argue debt into a sprint.
Recommendation: don't build a new skill yet; add a "debt/risk register health" check to `backlog-hygiene-auditor` (it already flags process-type rot) and let `tech-design-drafter`'s consequences section feed named debt items into the backlog through the normal decomposer path.

**6. What's rightly absent: the library avoided big-company ceremony — positive finding**
Evidence: no PI-planning machinery, no OKR cascade, no RACI generators, no cross-team velocity comparisons (explicitly banned in `sprint-report-generator` rules), no estimation automation (refinement-facilitator "never estimates itself"). The one ceremony-heavy artifact is the demo script with verified click-paths (`sprint-review-demo-facilitator` step 4) — which T4 showed will read "NEEDS VERIFICATION" on every line without env access.
Recommendation: keep the restraint. Slim the demo facilitator's verification step to "flag for the team to verify" as its default rather than its fallback.

### B. The PO pipeline

**7. The pipeline writes briefs to Confluence; the org keeps briefs in the epic Background field — resolve the contradiction in the org's favor — High leverage**
Evidence: `kdp-schema.md` "House epic content conventions": hand-authored epics carry brief-grade content in Background (`cf14757`) / Description / Requirements (`cf14762`), "consistent across authors"; T2: "the second epic in two tabletops whose Background field is ~90% of a brief — it's a house convention, not an accident." Yet `product-brief-builder` step 5 creates *only* a Confluence page, and T3 finding 13 shows why Confluence-resident context fails here: "Requirements live in Confluence, invisible from Jira… none of it linked from the Jira epics." The pipeline currently *reads* the house convention (brief reconstruction in brief-builder step 1, pipeline-adopter step 2) but refuses to *write* it — so pipeline-created epics will look structurally different from hand-created ones.
Recommendation: make the epic fields the primary brief surface. `product-brief-builder` (and `jira-confluence-writer` when creating epics) should write the brief content into Background/Description/Requirements in the house structure — kdp-schema already says "the house format is the contract" — with a Confluence page as the *optional* initiative-level umbrella for multi-epic efforts. This converts the brief from a document people must remember to open into content that lives where the work lives.

**8. The value stream shape is right, and pipeline-adopter is the load-bearing correction — positive finding, with one exposure — Medium leverage**
Evidence: Brief → Decompose → Write → DoR-check is a textbook value stream with drafts strictly separated from writes (decomposer "produces drafts only"; writer "create only; never edit"). The T1 shakedown correctly identified that a greenfield-only entrance fails a brownfield org, and `pipeline-adopter` (tiered reconciliation, "blocking a done item is theater", additive-over-destructive) is one of the most mature brownfield-adoption designs I've seen. The exposure: **the greenfield pipeline itself has never been live-fired** — T11 sits unscheduled in the candidates list while adoption ran live (docs/adoptions/2026-07-12-*). The flagship value stream is validated only on paper.
Recommendation: run T11 on the next real small initiative before promoting the pipeline to more teams. The T2 evidence (a real PO hand-built six stories with perfect house AC and *empty descriptions* in the same week) suggests the pipeline's real adoption competitor is the PO's existing habits — a live run is the only way to learn whether the interview cost is acceptable.

**9. Brief-lite degradation is the right pressure valve — positive finding — Low leverage**
Evidence: `backlog-decomposer` step 2 refuses to refuse: five-question intake, visible `⚠ Derived without approved brief` debt flag, DoR critic caps such stories at "Ready with notes." This is exactly how a gate survives deadline pressure without becoming theater.
Recommendation: none — but track how often brief-lite is used; if it becomes the majority path, the full brief interview is priced too high (see finding 18).

### C. Quality gates

**10. The DoR Critic is right-sized and the hotfix express contract is the best gate design in the library — positive finding — Medium leverage**
Evidence: the readiness contract ("could a tester or coding agent translate this story into test cases without inventing requirements?") is the correct bar for a Copilot-handoff shop; blocking/non-blocking item split, PO overrule-with-record, format tolerance ("substance blocks; style advises" — a T1 correction), the two-altitude epic checklist (T3's costliest lesson), and above all the H1–H4 hotfix express contract with its stated philosophy: "a rule that gets skipped under pressure protects nobody." That last sentence is the design principle the rest of the industry's DoD/DoR implementations lack.
Recommendation: apply the same compression thinking to the remaining full-ceremony paths (see findings 2, 17).

**11. The `dor-ready` label is a single point of failure for the developer handoff, in an instance where pipeline vocabulary doesn't exist yet — Medium leverage**
Evidence: T4 finding 11: "Zero pipeline vocabulary exists in the wild. No `dor-ready`… label appears anywhere in KDP." `copilot-handoff-packager` inputs require "refined, `dor-ready`, estimated by the team"; `implementation-planner` degrades gracefully (notes the missing label and lists gaps). Until the critic runs at scale, the developer skills' precondition is unsatisfiable, which invites teams to ignore the precondition permanently — the classic path to a bypassed gate.
Recommendation: give `copilot-handoff-packager` the planner's posture: when `dor-ready` is absent, run the invention test *as* the readiness check (it already does this in step 4) and record "packet passed invention test in lieu of DoR label" — the gate's substance without the unmet bureaucratic precondition.

**12. Gates guard artifact creation; the riskiest system writes (merge, deploy, close) stay outside the system — Medium leverage**
Evidence: every skill gates *its own output* (conventions.md: "Human approval gate… before any external write"). But the human merge decision (`code-review-critic`: "the developer owns the merge"), the deployment, and epic/initiative closure (finding 2) have no skill support at the moment of action. The system's risk model is "AI must not write ungated" — correct — but the org's risk lives at merge/deploy/close, where humans act *without* the evidence pack the skills could provide.
Recommendation: this is mostly findings 1–3 restated as a principle: extend the gate philosophy from "gate what AI writes" to "brief what humans decide." The skills' real product at merge/close/release time is the evidence table, not the approval.

### D. Developer and Tester roles

**13. The tester skill set is genuinely shift-left — but its automation surface is UI-only in a backend-heavy stack — Medium-High leverage**
Evidence of modernity: `test-plan-generator` runs "before refinement, to expose weak AC" and treats AC gaps as findings equal to the plan; `exploratory-charter-generator` paper mode routes requirements-shaped risks to the PO pre-build; `ac-playwright-scaffolder` delivers automation as code via PR with honest `fixme` markers ("a skeleton that admits what it doesn't know beats one that fakes a green run"). None of this is legacy QA-phase thinking. The gap: the only automation skill targets Playwright/UI, while the served stack is C# .NET + Node/TS with SQL Server and Cosmos — the dominant test layers are unit and API. `implementation-planner` step 3 classifies AC as "unit-testable… integration or Playwright" and then hands only the Playwright slice to a skill; the unit/API slice evaporates. `copilot-handoff-packager`'s DoD says "tests passing" with no skill defining what adequate tests are.
Recommendation: either generalize the scaffolder (an `ac-test-scaffolder` with Playwright/xUnit/Jest targets, same Given/When/Then mirroring and fixme honesty) or add an API-level sibling. For internal software this is where the escaped-defect leverage actually is — UI tests catch the least per dollar.

**14. CI health has no owner — Medium leverage**
Evidence: `daily-standup-digest` reads "CI failures on the main branch" as a signal and `code-review-critic` takes "CI status" as an input; no skill acts on a red main, flaky suites, or coverage decay. For a shop delegating implementation to the Copilot coding agent, CI is the *only* always-on gate — a red main silently invalidates every "tests passing" claim in every packet and PR description.
Recommendation: minimally, promote "main is red" to an act-now item in `impediment-radar` (it is an impediment — it blocks everyone). A full CI-health skill can wait for T6 (developer-role shakedown with repo access), which should be scheduled — the entire developer role remains paper-validated.

**15. Developer skills reflect modern practice; `incident-hotfix-runner` is the standout — positive finding — Low leverage**
Evidence: plan-before-code grounded in the real codebase ("if you haven't read the file, don't reference it"), delegation-fit honesty in `copilot-handoff-packager` step 2 (security-sensitive auth and risky migrations recommended *against* delegation — correct), AC-verbatim packets, review-the-tests-as-code rules, and a review checklist that is stack-specific and current (async/await misuse, Cosmos partition-key scans, React effect arrays). `incident-hotfix-runner` was *derived from observed practice* (Bug + fixVersion, not the dead hotfix types) rather than imposed — the right method.
Recommendation: none structural. Note `pr-hygiene` + `code-review-critic` + human review is three review passes on one PR; the skills manage the overlap well (pr-hygiene is "the cheap gate"), but watch run logs for developers skipping pr-hygiene as redundant — if they do, merge it into the critic as a pre-pass.

### E. Scrum Master role

**16. Eight SM skills are defensible — but as bookkeeping installers, not ceremony automators; say so and consolidate the shared engine — Medium leverage**
Evidence: T4's verdict is the key insight: the SM skills' failure mode on real data was "quiet dishonesty," and the fixes (commitment-baseline recipe, team operating record, data-sufficiency gate, first-run posture) turned them into instruments that *install the records the org lacks* — no commitment record has ever existed for AP Blue; the first `sprint-planning-facilitator` record-mode run "ends the archaeology." That's real value for a moderately mature org. However: `daily-standup-digest`, `impediment-radar`, and `sprint-report-generator` scan substantially the same signals (stalls 2+ days, aging reviews >1 day, flagged items, scope changes, hotfix-pattern items) with three separately-maintained rule texts — T5's incident-signal update had to be applied to all three.
Recommendation: extract the detection rules into one shared reference (`references/sprint-signals.md`, like `commitment-baseline.md`) that all three cite; keep the three skills as output surfaces (daily one-screen, escalation-grade, stakeholder-grade). Also run T14 (the live first-run pair) on schedule — it is time-sensitive and converts the whole T4 correction set from proposals into tested behavior.

**17. The daily approval gate on the standup digest is the library's most likely fatigue point — Medium leverage**
Evidence: `daily-standup-digest` step 4 requires SM review every morning before posting, for an artifact that is read-only analysis with neutral-phrasing rules baked in. The uniform-gate convention (conventions.md) is what demands this; the DAAP-bot cautionary tale (T4 finding 13) is the justification. But a gate approved 10 times a sprint by reflex protects less than a gate approved once with attention — the PO guide itself warns "rubber-stamping… turns the safety model into theater."
Recommendation: introduce a *standing approval* tier for recurring, read-only, template-pinned artifacts: the SM approves the digest's format and posting destination once per sprint; daily runs post automatically with the run log as audit, and any rule firing outside normal bounds (e.g., first hotfix swarm of the sprint) re-triggers the per-run gate. Keep per-run gates everywhere the artifact makes claims about people, scope, or externals (escalations, reports, retro packs).

**18. No detection of gate quality — the run logs could measure rubber-stamping and interview cost, and don't — Medium leverage**
Evidence: `templates/run-log.md` captures approval decisions, revisions, and verbatim Q&A; `skill-author`'s audit checklist audits *skills*, not *runs*. Nothing computes: approval latency, % of gates passed with zero edits (rubber-stamp signal), questions per interview (cost signal), or brief-lite usage share (finding 9).
Recommendation: add a run-log sweep to `skill-author`'s audit workflow (or a fourth workflow): quarterly, aggregate the `.ai-sdlc/runs/` corpus into gate-health metrics. This is the cheapest possible instrumentation because the data is already being written.

### F. Jira/Confluence usage

**19. Stop accommodating; start remediating — the system needs a channel to Jira admins — High leverage**
Evidence: the accumulated accommodations are now a tax on every skill: the 16-entry "done-category ≠ terminal" trap registry (`kdp-workflows.md`) that every counting skill must obey; zombie sprints carried in per-team ignore lists (team-operating-record) instead of being closed once; the Flagged field treated as "a claim, not a fact" with 50+ relic flags nobody clears; three dead hotfix types "remain in Jira's registry pending a team decision to retire" (`kdp-bug-types.md`) — a decision no artifact requests; dead team-field values ("DO NOT USE", departed staff) detected forever rather than deleted once. The shakedowns *found* all of this; the findings route to POs and SMs who cannot change schemes.
Recommendation: create a standing **instance remediation backlog** — one document (or JSM tickets to the Jira admin team) generated from the instance-intelligence files, each entry naming the accommodation it retires: (a) close the zombie sprint containers (one-time, ends the ignore lists); (b) bulk-clear relic flags (one-time, makes the Flagged field usable — note `impediment-radar` already *re-seeds* it correctly with evidence comments); (c) retire the 3 hotfix types, and probably the dormant Clarification type (14 ever) and the fossilized Risk usage; (d) fix the resolution screens (below). The hygiene auditor is the natural owner of proposing these; the missing piece is only the addressee.

**20. The workflow scheme itself is the root cause of the counting problem — propose recategorization — High leverage**
Evidence: PO Validated, Ready to Deploy To QA, Deployed to QA, Regression Passed, and even Regression *Failed* are done-*category* statuses (`kdp-workflows.md` trap registry: "a FAILURE state in done clothing"); several true terminals never set resolution (Design Story Closed, UAT Story Closed, all observed hotfix Dones). Consequence: Jira's native reports, every third-party dashboard, and any future tool are wrong by construction — the library's terminal-set rule is a workaround only this library knows.
Recommendation: the remediation backlog's flagship items: recategorize the mid-pipeline waypoints to In Progress category, and add resolution post-functions/screens to every terminal transition. Two admin changes; afterwards `statusCategory=Done` becomes truthful, the trap registry demotes to history, and every counting rule in eight skills simplifies. This is the single highest-leverage Jira change available to this org.

**21. Concede the story-points battle formally; make flow metrics primary — Medium-High leverage**
Evidence: 81% of AP Blue's recently completed items carry no points (T4); the convention "bugs don't get points" is consistent and now recorded in the team operating record; the skills already fall back to throughput with the "not computable" label, and `commitment-baseline.md` already treats changelogs as the only truth. The library currently frames throughput as the degraded mode and points-velocity as the target state.
Recommendation: invert the default for this org: changelog-derived flow metrics (throughput by type, cycle time per status, carryover rate, unplanned-work share) as the primary planning/reporting evidence, points as optional corroboration where a team maintains them. This matches modern practice for internal delivery teams (flow metrics over estimation ceremonies) and stops the skills from implying the org should backfill a pointing discipline it has already voted against. The data plumbing (changelog trust) is already built.

**22. A simpler issue-type scheme would serve better — propose it, don't just map it — Medium leverage**
Evidence: 24 KDP types, of which the pipeline's own registry shows roughly ten are dead or dormant: 3 hotfix types (5 issues ever), Clarification (14 ever, mostly 2020–22), Risk (one fossil register), the UAT Story/Test Case family (one-shot campaign artifacts, "exactly 2 issues have ever had a populated AC field" — T5), Task ("avoid — prefer a specific type"). The living scheme is: Initiative, Epic, Story, Tech Managed ×2, Spike, Sub-task, Story Bug, Bug — nine types that carry essentially all real work.
Recommendation: include type rationalization in the remediation backlog: retire the dead types; replace the UAT family with standard Story/Bug + a `uat` label + the campaign-epic pattern the org already invented (T5: "the traceability instinct is present — it just lives in link types"). Fewer types means every skill's type-selection logic, the DoR's type-aware framing rules, and new-hire comprehension all get cheaper. This is the "fundamental change" question answered: yes — the system should drive this, because it is now the only actor in the org with the empirical map to argue it.

### G. Metrics and feedback loops

**23. The system has a qualitative improvement loop that demonstrably works, and no quantitative one — Medium-High leverage**
Evidence: the shakedown → skill-author → template-change loop closed dozens of findings in two days (scenario-backlog statuses; commit history). But nothing measures outcomes: no escaped-defect trend, no bug-noise rate over time (T3's 37% non-fix closure rate is the obvious baseline already computed), no AC-coverage-at-sprint-entry trend (T1's 30-of-46 and T4's 13-of-26 are baselines), no cycle-time before/after, no rework rate. The `ai-sdlc-generated`/`ai-sdlc-adopted` labels create a natural cohort comparison (pipeline-born vs. hand-made items: DoR pass rate, story-bug count, time-in-validation) that nobody exploits.
Recommendation: define 4–5 system-scoreboard metrics, all computable from data the library already trusts: (1) % of sprint-entering items with AC (per team, per type-convention); (2) bug non-fix closure rate per area; (3) changelog cycle time through the validation states; (4) hotfix trace completeness (incident↔fix↔regressed-story links present); (5) labeled-cohort DoR pass rate. Publish them in the sprint report or a quarterly system-health page. Without this, the claim "the system helps" rests forever on anecdote — and the first budget conversation will ask.

### H. Human-in-the-loop calibration

**24. Risk-tiering of gates has started organically; finish it deliberately — Medium leverage**
Evidence of good calibration already present: hotfix express (4 items, minutes), pipeline-adopter's three tiers with bulk approval at the owner's discretion, hygiene scale mode (category-level decisions above ~30 findings), digest private mode. Evidence of remaining uniformity: conventions.md mandates the same gate shape for a Confluence retro pack as for a production-adjacent Jira write; the readiness *report* needs PO approval even before anything is posted (two decisions — "is the analysis right" and "may it be written" — fused into one gate).
Recommendation: codify a three-tier gate taxonomy in `conventions.md`: **per-item gates** for edits to existing human content and anything person-adjacent (escalations, review comments); **per-run gates** for new-artifact creation (current default); **standing approvals** for recurring read-only template-pinned artifacts (finding 17). The audit checklist then enforces that each skill declares its tier and why. This preserves the DAAP-bot lesson (nothing ungated) while spending human attention where the risk is.

**25. Self-approval blind spot: the PO approves the critique of their own stories — Low leverage**
Evidence: `definition-of-ready-critic` step 5: the PO — who authored or commissioned the stories — approves the readiness report and "can overrule any finding." The overrule is recorded (good), but the gate is structurally self-review; under sprint pressure a PO can green their own backlog. The same person also approves the brief, the decomposition, and the write plan — four consecutive gates, one approver.
Recommendation: cheap fix, no new ceremony: the readiness report (with any overrules) is already designed to be posted to Jira — make the *team's sight of it in refinement* the explicit backstop, and have `refinement-facilitator` prep cards surface "PO-overruled DoR findings" as a named line. Sunlight, not a second approver.

## Fundamental changes to consider

1. **Move the brief into Jira.** Adopt the house Background/Description/Requirements convention as the brief's primary home; demote Confluence to the optional initiative umbrella. The org already made this decision by practice; the pipeline should stop maintaining a parallel convention it must then reconcile (finding 7).

2. **Open a remediation channel to Jira admins and spend it on three changes:** recategorize the done-category waypoint statuses + enforce resolution on terminal transitions; close the zombie sprints and clear the relic flags; retire the ~10 dead issue types. Together these delete the majority of the instance-intelligence workaround burden and make the org's own Jira reporting truthful for the first time — the system's accommodations then become the migration bridge they should be, not permanent architecture (findings 19, 20, 22).

3. **Complete the gate map at the back of the lifecycle:** a Definition of Done + PO-validation assist at story exit, an epic-closeout gate at epic exit, and a release-readiness audit at fixVersion exit. The library's front gates are excellent; every expensive recorded failure in the shakedowns happened at an exit (findings 1–3).

4. **Make flow metrics the system's native language** — changelog-derived throughput and cycle time as primary, points as corroboration — and stand up the system's own scoreboard (AC coverage, bug noise, trace completeness, labeled-cohort comparison) so the "is this helping?" question has a quantitative answer within two quarters (findings 21, 23).

5. **Tier the approval gates by risk** (per-item / per-run / standing) and instrument gate health from the run logs already being written. The uniform-gate rule was right for launch; approval fatigue is now the bigger threat to the safety model than an ungated write (findings 17, 18, 24).

6. **Consolidate the SM detection engine and generalize the test scaffolder** — one shared sprint-signals reference feeding digest/radar/report, and AC-to-test scaffolding that covers xUnit/Jest/API as well as Playwright, because the served stack's defect mass is below the UI (findings 13, 16).

7. **Live-fire what's still paper:** T11 (greenfield PO pipeline with writes), T14 (the time-sensitive S89→S90 commitment-record boundary), and T6 (developer role against a real repo). The library's credibility rests on its empirical method — its two flagship value streams (greenfield PO, developer handoff) are the last things validated only on paper.
